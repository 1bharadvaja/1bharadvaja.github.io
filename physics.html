First Problem You'll Be Tackling

A pen of unit length is lying tilted on a rough table.

The lower end of the pen is dragged in the horizontal direction until the pen becomes vertical. During this process, the upper end of the pen travels a unit distance.

How many units does the lower end travel?

Assume that the velocity of the upper end of the pen is always parallel to the pen.

Answer

First thing we can do, is draw a mini diagram of this.

In this picture, we need to find x, which is simple found by integrating dx, but we dont know the full bounds of integration.

We can substitute ${\mathrm{d}x}$ for $\csc\theta {\mathrm{d} \theta }$. We then integrate $\int_{\theta =a}^{\pi \over2}\csc\theta {\mathrm{d} \theta }$. We do not know the lower bound of the integral, therefore we need to find it by using another integral.

We have to use another integral, at this point, to find the lower bound for the integral. We know that the top of the pen moves 1 unit. This means P = 1. You can set 1 = $\int_{a}^{\pi \over 2}{\mathrm{d}p} = \int_{a}^{\pi \over 2} \cot \theta {\mathrm{d} \theta } $. This is because in the diagram, $\cot \theta =$${\mathrm{d} p} \over {\mathrm{d} \theta }$, so therefore $\cot \theta {\mathrm{d} \theta } = {\mathrm{d} p}$.

$\int_{a}^{\pi \over 2} \cot \theta {\mathrm{d} \theta } = \ln |\sin \frac{\pi }{2}|-\ln |\sin \alpha |$

$\ln |\sin \frac{\pi }{2}|-\ln |\sin \alpha | = 1$. Then you can solve, $\ln |\sin \frac{\pi }{2}| = 0$ and $0-\ln |\sin \alpha | = 1$

Since $-\ln |\sin \alpha | = 1$, $e^{-1} = \sin \theta$, so therefore, $\theta = \arcsin (\frac{1}{e})$. Now we have found out the lower bound.

Now we can evaluate the first integral. $\int_{\arcsin (\frac{1}{e})}^{\pi \over2}\csc\theta {\mathrm{d} \theta }$.

This evaluates to $-\ln|\csc \frac{\pi}{2} + \cot \frac{\pi}{2}| + \ln|\csc (\arcsin (\frac{1}{e}))+ \cot (\arcsin (\frac{1}{e}))|$. At this point, we can draw a triangle to figure out the values.

As you can see, we have put out the values on the triangle, and now, we have to find out the cosecant and cotangentvalues. According to this triangle, $\csc \theta = e$, and then $\cot \theta = \sqrt{e^{2}-1}$.

This gives you the answer of $\ln|e + \sqrt{e^{2}-1}|$. And the problem is solved!

Second Problem

Prove the relationship between Gauss's Law for electricity and Coulomb's Law.

Answer

Okay, so this proof is fairly straight forward. Gauss's Law states that $\epsilon_{0}\int\vec{E}\cdot{\mathrm{d}A} = q_{enc}$, while Coulombs Law states $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \frac{q_{1}}{r^{2}}$.

Gauss's law conceptually states that the net electric flux is proportional to the net electrical charge by a constant of proportionality of $\frac {1}{\epsilon_{0}}$, while Coulomb's Law conceptually states that electric force and electric field strength are inversely proportional to $r^{2}$.

Now we can go about proving the relationship.

$\epsilon_{0}\int\vec{E}\cdot{\mathrm{d}A} = q_{enc}$. We can take out the electric field vector $\vec{E}$, because it is the same regardless of where it is on the surface. Now our integral is $\epsilon_{0}E\int{\mathrm{d}A}$.

Now the integral is simply the sum of all the differntials of the sphere, which happens to be the surface area, or $4\pi r^{2}$.

This gives us the equation $\epsilon_{0}E(4\pi r^{2}) = q_{enc}$. Some rearranging gives us the formula $E = \frac{q_{enc}}{4\pi r^{2}\epsilon_{0}}$, and the relationship is thus proved.

Third Problem

Consider a three-dimensional shape standing on the floor with height $\frac{\pi}{2} \text{cm}$ such that the area of a cross section parallel to the floor is $14\sin x\cdot \cos x \text{ cm}^2$ for any height $0 \le x \le \frac{\pi}{2}\text{ cm}.$ What is the volume of the three-dimensional shape in $\text{cm}^3$?

Answer

You know the three dimensions of the solid which are $14\sin x$, $\cos x$, and the height is $\frac{\pi}{2}$.

Now you can integrate to find out the to find out the volume. You sum up infinitely many pieces of height ${\mathrm{d} x}$, and with length and and width of $14\sin x$ and $\cos x$ respectively. The bounds of integration will be from 0 to $\frac{\pi}{2}$

Integrating $\int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}14\sin x \cdot \cos x$ gives us the result of 7, and that is our answer.

Fourth Problem

This problem was created by a brilliant.org user named Michael Ng. All credit goes to him for this problem.

A bullet is formed by revolving the area bounded by the the curve $y = \ln(x)$ from $x = 1$ to $x = e$ about the $x$-axis.

It is then shot straight into a very thick wall (i.e. it does not pierce through the other side at all) making a closed cylindrical hole until it stops moving.

Then the bullet is carefully extracted without affecting the hole at all, leaving an empty hole with a pointy end where the bullet once was. The length of the entire hole is $e + 1$.

If the volume of the hole can be expressed $\pi i e,$ as where $i$ is a constant, find the value of $i$.

Answer

The plan is to find the volume first, then divide the volume by $\pi$ and $e$ to get to $i$.

Since the curve is $\ln(x)$ from from $x=1$ to $x=e$, we can graph this portion and revolve it around the $x$-axis to get the shape of the bullet.

Once that is done, you know the length of the bullet to be $e-1$. This means that after hitting the point $(e-1, y)$ (y is the value of the function at $e-1$, but it is not needed in this problem), in the wall, the height of the hole is the same until $e+1$.

You can represent this as a sum of two integrals. By using the disc method, we know that until the point $(e-1, y)$, the height or radius of the circle is going to be $\ln(x)$, and after that point, the height will be constant until the point $(e+1, z)$, where the hole ends.

The two integrals will be $\pi\int_{0}^{e-1}{(\ln(x))}^2 {\mathrm {d}x} + \pi\int_{e-1}^{e+1}{0.54132}^2 {\mathrm {d}x}$. In this case, $0.54132$ is the constant height of the function after hitting $e-1$.

If you don't know the disc method, go to the brilliant.org amazing wiki where you can learn about it.

Continuing with the problem, the sum of integrals evaluates to approximately $8.37496327997$, but remember that is not our answer.

We have to find the constant $i$, which is basically our answer divided by $\pi e$. So we perform our calculations and we get $0.98$, and w round that to $1$, and that is our answer.
