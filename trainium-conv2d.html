Optimizing a Conv2D Kernel on AWS Trainium

May 29, 2026 · ~13 min read · NKI / NeuronCore-v2

The target is a single AWS Trainium 1 chip: one NeuronCore-v2, with a 128×128 systolic Tensor Engine. The job is a 2D convolution written in NKI, AWS's low-level kernel language for Neuron hardware. There's a leaderboard. Each test case has a latency threshold, your score is how far under it you land, and there are eleven visible shapes: a mix of float16 and float32, filters of 3×3 and 5×5, batch 4 and 16.

I started at a cold baseline of 4360µs summed across the fleet, with a mean score of 1.7892. The leaderboard top was 1.8427. I ended at 3371µs and 1.8392, which is 989µs shaved and about 93% of the gap to first place closed. This is the story of those 989 microseconds. It comes in two parts: a first round of "play by the hardware's rules" optimizations that reached a comfortable local optimum, and then a structural rewrite plus a precision trick that broke out of it.

The hardware has opinions

Trainium is not a GPU and it will not forgive you for pretending it is. Three constraints shaped every decision:

  • DMA contiguity. When you nl.load a tile from HBM, every dimension except the first has to form a contiguous buffer in memory. Break this and the compiler doesn't error. It silently emits thousands of scalar loads, and you eat a hard latency floor.
  • Eight PSUM banks. The accumulator memory has exactly eight banks per core. Ask for sixteen and you get PSUM8 — allocated memory out of bound. This caps how many matmul accumulators can be live at once, which caps how well you can pipeline.
  • A scalar processor that stalls the matrix pipeline. Integer division and modulo run on a slow scalar unit. Put them in an inner loop and they stall the Tensor Engine for microseconds at a time.

Almost everything below is some version of tripping one of these rules and then rearranging the kernel to stop.

Part one: playing by the rules

Load the weights once, globally

The intuitive thing is to load each weight tile right before you use it. On Trainium that forces the large input tensor X to be re-read from HBM on every weight pass. Bulk-hoisting all the weights into SBUF (the on-chip scratchpad) at the very top means X gets streamed exactly once per compute block. The early bottleneck was HBM bandwidth, not compute, so this is the single biggest lever on it.

The 128-loop that dodges a silent transpose

I wanted the weights laid out transposed in SBUF, and the natural move is to let a 2D DMA mapping do the transpose implicitly. The compiler accepted it, then quietly emitted 6,400 scalar load instructions and pinned me to a ~600µs floor. The DMA engine won't treat a non-contiguous free dimension as a bulk transfer.

The fix was to stop being clever: pull contiguous 3D strips with an explicit loop and scatter them into the stationary tiles by hand.

weight load
# contiguous 3D strips [128 channels, H, W], scattered manually
for om in nl.static_range(128):
    w_strip = nl.load(W[oct_*OC + om, ict*P + w_k, w_h, w_w])
    for kh, kw:
        weight_tiles_ci[kh][kw][k_i, om + k_j] = w_strip[k_i, kh, kw + k_j]

Ugly, but it stays on the bulk DMA path. Part two deletes it entirely for something much better.

Cache the view, slice once

For the 256-output-channel cases the kernel computes two output tiles at once. I'd been slicing the moving input view twice, once per accumulator. Caching the slice into a local and feeding both accumulators halved the NKI tracer's slicing overhead.

view caching
v0 = row_block[v_c, v_r, v_w + kw]   # slice once...
acc0 += nisa.nc_matmul(w0, v0)        # ...reuse for both OC tiles
acc1 += nisa.nc_matmul(w1, v0)

Relatedly, loading the exact row block each filter row needs makes a width shift by kw a zero-cost pointer offset instead of a strided gather that triggers a hidden tensor_copy.

Unroll the 3×3, and stop dividing

Two instruction-level wins. For the hot 3×3 cases I deleted the kh/kw loops and emitted nine explicit nc_matmul calls, which removes loop-control latency in favor of a flat block of matrix math.

The second one is less obvious. I'd tried flattening batch and spatial dims into one 1D loop and recovering the indices with // and %. Because the NKI stack allocator forces you to skip the LLVM middle-end, those divisions ran raw on the scalar processor and stalled the matmul pipeline by ~10µs. Plain nested affine_range loops let the hardware's native loop controller take over for free. On this stack the "smart" flattening is slower than the dumb nested loop, because the smartness compiles to scalar arithmetic the matrix engine has to wait on.

The decorator stack that holds this together looks wrong on purpose:

the kernel contract
@ncc.enable_stack_allocator
@ncc.skip_middle_end_transformations
@nki.jit
def conv2d(X, W, bias, ...):
    ...

Skipping the middle-end optimizer sounds insane until you notice that with manual unrolling and native loops, the AST is already the schedule you want. Letting the optimizer touch it just invited it to software-pipeline the dual accumulators into a PSUM overflow.

That got me to a clean local optimum. To actually move past it I had to throw out the weight-loading scheme.

Part two: the unlock

One HBM load instead of 128

The hand-scattered 128-loop from part one was correct but expensive: 128 separate DMA descriptors, each scattering one strip across 128 output positions. The rewrite, which I called the D2.1 hoist, does a single 4D block load whose last three dimensions cover the entire (ic, fh, fw) sub-cube, so contiguity holds, and then transposes each tap on the Tensor Engine.

D2.1 weight hoist
oc_p = nl.arange(TILE_CO)[:, None, None, None]
ic_f = nl.arange(TILE_CI)[None, :, None, None]
kh_f = nl.arange(filter_height)[None, None, :, None]
kw_f = nl.arange(filter_width)[None, None, None, :]

# one contiguous 4D block -- par_dim=CO, free=(CI,FH,FW)
w_block = nl.load(W[co_t*TILE_CO + oc_p, ic_t*TILE_CI + ic_f, kh_f, kw_f])

w_buf = nl.ndarray((nl.par_dim(TILE_CI), FH, FW, TILE_CO), dtype=W.dtype, buffer=nl.sbuf)
for kk in nl.affine_range(FH * FW):    # affine, not static -- avoids unrolling all 9/25 at once
    kh_s, kw_s = kk // FW, kk % FW
    wt = nisa.nc_transpose(w_block[:, :, kh_s, kw_s])
    w_buf[:, kh_s, kw_s, :] = nisa.tensor_copy(wt)

The nine (or twenty-five) on-chip transposes cost about 0.4µs total, which is nothing next to the DMA serialization they replace. Using affine_range over the taps instead of nested static_range matters: it stops the compiler from unrolling every transpose at once, which had been blowing up PSUM live-ranges.

Actually using all eight PSUM banks

The automatic allocator was effectively using four of the eight PSUM banks. Explicitly placing an 8-deep accumulator forces all eight into distinct physical banks.

8-bank residency
acc_buf = nl.ndarray(
    (8, nl.par_dim(TILE_CO), ROW_BLOCK, out_width),
    dtype=nl.float32,
    buffer=ncc.psum.mod_alloc(base_bank=0, num_bank_tiles=(8,)),
)

With eight accumulators live, the next eight nc_matmuls issue back-to-back without bank stalls, and the drain of one accumulator (a vector op plus a DMA store) overlaps with the matmul feeding the next. That's the difference between a pipeline with bubbles and one without. Asking for sixteen banks confirmed the ceiling: NCC_INLA001 — out of bound PSUM8.

Octet X reuse, and an 18-row super-tile

When both output-channel tiles share the same input, iterating output-channel-outer makes you load X twice. Restructuring to load X once per "octet" of eight blocks and letting both OC passes reuse it cuts those DMAs in half. For the 66×66 case I could also fuse the loads for two adjacent row-blocks into one 18-row super-tile. I swept 10 / 18 / 34 / 66 rows; 18 (exactly 2 × ROW_BLOCK) was the sweet spot, overlapping each DMA with several matmuls instead of blocking the stream on one big load.

Spending the error budget

The last real lever was precision. The matmul cost on NeuronCore-v2 is simple:

cycles per nc_matmul
fp32            : 4 * max(min(64, N_stat), N_moving)
fp16/bf16/tf32  :     max(min(64, N_stat), N_moving)

An fp32 matmul costs 4× a TF32 one, so every fp32 tap I can demote to TF32 saves three-quarters of that tap's cycles. The catch is correctness. The fp32 test tolerance is rtol=1e-5, and TF32's 10-bit mantissa introduces about 1e-3 relative error per multiply, so you don't get many taps before you blow the budget.

So I treated the tolerance as a budget. A separate harness runs the kernel against PyTorch's conv2d across 30 RNG seeds drawn from the tester's exact input distribution, and records the worst-case ratio of observed error to allowed tolerance.

stress_check.py
diff       = np.abs(Y_kernel - Y_torch)        # per-element error
abs_budget = atol + rtol * np.abs(Y_torch)   # per-element tolerance
rel_use    = (diff / abs_budget).max()        # worst over all positions
# rel_use = 1.0 means we exactly graze the ceiling at the worst pixel

My rule was to only accept anything over ~0.7 of budget for a sizeable win, and to reject anything grazing 1.0. A single TF32 corner tap on the fp32 3×3 case lands at 0.91, right on the edge but passing 30 seeds, so it stays. A second full TF32 tap doubles the error to ~180% and fails outright. I tested four positions for that second tap; all of them fail. One tap it is.

Case D is the interesting one, because it has two input-channel tiles. That means I can apply TF32 to ci_t=0 only and leave the ci_t=1 matmuls in pure fp32. Each output pixel's error then scales with how many TF32 taps hit the first tile, not the total, so the budget stretches a lot further.

Each tap is worth about 10µs. A fifth would cross 100% on at least one seed, so I stopped at four. The X-side cast had its own wrinkle: caching all eight batches in TF32 overflowed SBUF by 832 bytes, so I used a single 2KB shared buffer that gets overwritten per batch, since the Tensor Engine consumes it right after each cast anyway.

shared TF32 scratch
x_tf32 = nl.ndarray((nl.par_dim(TILE_CI), ROW_BLOCK, out_width),
                  dtype=nl.tfloat32, buffer=nl.sbuf)
for s in nl.static_range(8):
    x_tf32[...] = nisa.tensor_copy(bigs[s][v_c, kh + v_r, v_w + kw], dtype=nl.tfloat32)
    acc_buf[s] += nisa.nc_matmul(Wt_tf32, x_tf32)   # consumed before next overwrite

This is what took the mean score from 1.8361 to 1.8392.

Stuff that didn't work

  • Mixed-dtype matmul (TF32 weights, fp32 input), rejected with NCC_IBIR034. Both operands must share precision, which is why a TF32 tap means casting both sides.
  • PE tiling, splitting the 128-row stationary matrix into two 64-row tiles. Cost is bound by max(…, N_moving), so splitting the stationary dimension buys nothing when the moving dimension is full-size. It cost +150µs.
  • Multi-core SPMD. kernel[nl.nc(2)](...) fails on trn1 with "SPMD grid with multi neuron cores is not supported." That's a trn2 feature.
  • Tuning instruction order. Block-order, drain-order and tap-order sweeps were all ±2µs noise, because skip_middle_end_transformations schedules matmuls from the dependency graph, not source order.
  • An 8th TF32 tap on the 5×5 case, which passed stress at 97% of budget. Technically a pass, but 97% across 30 seeds is the kind of thing that fails on seed 31, so I capped it at 7.
