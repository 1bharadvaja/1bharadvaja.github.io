Optimizing a Conv2D Kernel on AWS Trainium

May 29, 2026 · ~14 min read · NKI / NeuronCore-v2

A play-by-play of taking a Trainium NKI convolution kernel from a sluggish baseline down to sub-400µs-per-case leaderboard territory — a fight against DMA contiguity rules, an 8-bank PSUM ceiling, scalar-processor stalls, and an absurdly tight floating-point error budget that I learned to spend like a currency.

00The setup

The target is a single AWS Trainium 1 chip — one NeuronCore-v2, a 128×128 systolic Tensor Engine — and the job is a 2D convolution written in NKI, AWS's low-level kernel language for Neuron hardware. There's a leaderboard. Each test case has a latency threshold, and your score is how far under it you land. Eleven visible shapes, a mix of float16 and float32, filter sizes 3×3 and 5×5, batch 4 and 16.

I started at a cold baseline of 4360µs summed across the fleet and a mean score of 1.7892. The leaderboard top was 1.8427. That 0.0535 gap doesn't sound like much until you realize it's the difference between "wrote a correct conv" and "wrung the hardware dry." Here's where it ended up:

The rest of this post is the story of those 989 microseconds. It splits cleanly into two acts: the first round of "play by the hardware's rules" optimizations that got me to a comfortable local optimum, and then the structural rewrite plus a precision trick that broke out of it.

01The hardware actually has opinions

Before any of the clever stuff, you have to internalize that Trainium is not a GPU and will not forgive you for pretending it is. Three constraints shaped every decision:

  • DMA contiguity. When you nl.load a tile from HBM, every dimension except the first must form a contiguous buffer in memory. Violate this and the compiler doesn't error — it silently emits thousands of scalar loads and you eat a hard latency floor.
  • 8 PSUM banks. The accumulator memory has exactly 8 banks per core. Ask for 16 and you get PSUM8 — allocated memory out of bound. This caps how many matmul accumulators can be live at once, which caps your pipelining.
  • A scalar processor that stalls the matrix pipeline. Integer division and modulo run raw on a slow scalar unit. Do them in your inner loop and they'll stall the Tensor Engine for microseconds at a time.

Every "optimization" below is really just me discovering one of these rules the hard way and rearranging the kernel to stop tripping it.

02Act one — playing by the rules

Load the weights once, globally

The intuitive thing is to load each weight tile right before you need it. On Trainium that's a trap: it forces the giant input tensor X to be re-read from HBM on every weight pass. The fix is to bulk-hoist all the weights into SBUF (the on-chip scratchpad) at the very top, so X is streamed exactly once per compute block. HBM bandwidth, not compute, was the early bottleneck — and this is the single biggest lever on it.

The 128-loop that dodges a silent transpose

I wanted the weights laid out transposed in SBUF. The natural move is to let a 2D DMA mapping do the transpose implicitly. The compiler accepted it — and quietly emitted 6,400 scalar load instructions, pinning me to a ~600µs floor. The DMA engine simply refuses to treat a non-contiguous free dimension as a bulk transfer.

The workaround was to stop being clever and pull contiguous 3D strips with an explicit loop, scattering them into the stationary tiles by hand:

python · weight load
# contiguous 3D strips [128 channels, H, W], scattered manually
for om in nl.static_range(128):
    w_strip = nl.load(W[oct_*OC + om, ict*P + w_k, w_h, w_w])
    for kh, kw:
        weight_tiles_ci[kh][kw][k_i, om + k_j] = w_strip[k_i, kh, kw + k_j]

Ugly, but it stays on the bulk DMA path. (Spoiler: act two deletes this entirely in favor of something much better.)

Cache the view, slice once

For the 256-output-channel cases the kernel computes two output tiles at once. I'd originally sliced the moving input view twice — once per accumulator. Caching the slice into a local and feeding both accumulators halved the NKI tracer's slicing overhead:

python · view caching
v0 = row_block[v_c, v_r, v_w + kw]   # slice once…
acc0 += nisa.nc_matmul(w0, v0)        # …reuse for both OC tiles
acc1 += nisa.nc_matmul(w1, v0)

Related: I learned to load the exact row block each filter row needs, so that shifting the width by kw is a zero-cost pointer offset rather than a strided gather that triggers a hidden tensor_copy.

Unroll the 3×3, and stop dividing

Two instruction-level wins. First, for the hot 3×3 cases I deleted the kh/kw loops entirely and emitted nine explicit nc_matmul calls — a flat, dense block of matrix math with zero loop-control latency.

Second, and more subtly: I'd tried flattening batch and spatial dims into one 1D loop, then recovering the indices with // and %. Because the NKI stack allocator makes you skip the LLVM middle-end, those divisions ran raw on the scalar processor and stalled the matmul pipeline by ~10µs. Reverting to plain nested affine_range loops let the hardware's zero-cost native loop controller take over.

The lesson On this stack, the "smart" loop flattening is slower than the dumb nested loop, because the smartness compiles to scalar arithmetic the matrix engine has to wait on. Let the hardware count.

The decorator stack that ties this together is deliberately counterintuitive:

python · the kernel contract
@ncc.enable_stack_allocator
@ncc.skip_middle_end_transformations
@nki.jit
def conv2d(X, W, bias, ...):
    ...

Skipping the middle-end optimizer sounds insane until you realize that with manual unrolling and native loops, the AST is already the schedule you want. Letting the optimizer touch it just invited it to software-pipeline the dual accumulators into a PSUM overflow. Sometimes the best thing the compiler can do is keep its hands off.

This got me to a clean local optimum. Good, not great. To actually move I had to throw out the weight-loading scheme.

03Act two — the unlock

One HBM load instead of 128

That hand-scattered 128-loop from act one was correct but expensive: 128 separate DMA descriptors, each scattering one strip across 128 output positions. The rewrite — I called it the D2.1 hoist — does a single 4D block load whose last three dimensions cover the entire (ic, fh, fw) sub-cube, so contiguity holds, and then transposes each tap on the Tensor Engine:

python · D2.1 weight hoist
oc_p = nl.arange(TILE_CO)[:, None, None, None]
ic_f = nl.arange(TILE_CI)[None, :, None, None]
kh_f = nl.arange(filter_height)[None, None, :, None]
kw_f = nl.arange(filter_width)[None, None, None, :]

# one contiguous 4D block — par_dim=CO, free=(CI,FH,FW)
w_block = nl.load(W[co_t*TILE_CO + oc_p, ic_t*TILE_CI + ic_f, kh_f, kw_f])

w_buf = nl.ndarray((nl.par_dim(TILE_CI), FH, FW, TILE_CO), dtype=W.dtype, buffer=nl.sbuf)
for kk in nl.affine_range(FH * FW):    # not static_range — avoids unrolling all 9/25 at once
    kh_s, kw_s = kk // FW, kk % FW
    wt = nisa.nc_transpose(w_block[:, :, kh_s, kw_s])
    w_buf[:, kh_s, kw_s, :] = nisa.tensor_copy(wt)

The nine (or twenty-five) on-chip transposes cost about 0.4µs total — completely negligible next to the DMA serialization they replace. Using affine_range over the taps instead of nested static_range was load-bearing: it stops the compiler from unrolling every transpose simultaneously, which had been blowing up PSUM live-ranges.

This one change was worth −874µs across the fleet. It's the whole ballgame:

Actually using all 8 PSUM banks

The automatic allocator was effectively using 4 of the 8 PSUM banks. Explicitly placing an 8-deep accumulator forces all eight into distinct physical banks:

python · 8-bank residency
acc_buf = nl.ndarray(
    (8, nl.par_dim(TILE_CO), ROW_BLOCK, out_width),
    dtype=nl.float32,
    buffer=ncc.psum.mod_alloc(base_bank=0, num_bank_tiles=(8,)),
)

With eight accumulators live, the next eight nc_matmuls issue back-to-back without bank stalls, and the drain of one accumulator (a vector op plus a DMA store) overlaps with the matmul feeding the next. This is the difference between a pipeline with bubbles and one without. Trying for 16 banks confirmed the ceiling: NCC_INLA001 — out of bound PSUM8.

Octet-outer X reuse, and an 18-row super-tile

When both output-channel tiles share the same input, iterating output-channel-outer makes you load X twice. Restructuring to load X once per "octet" of eight blocks and letting both OC passes reuse it cut those DMAs in half. And for the 66×66 case I could fuse the loads for two adjacent row-blocks into a single 18-row super-tile — I swept 10 / 18 / 34 / 66 rows and 18 (exactly 2 × ROW_BLOCK) was the sweet spot, overlapping each DMA with several matmuls instead of blocking the stream on one mega-load.

These are single-digit-microsecond wins each, but they're free and they compound.

04Spending the error budget

Here's the part I'm proudest of, because it's less "optimization" and more "exploiting the grader's tolerance as a resource."

The matmul cost formula on NeuronCore-v2 is brutally simple:

cycles per nc_matmul
fp32            : 4 * max(min(64, N_stat), N_moving)
fp16/bf16/tf32  :     max(min(64, N_stat), N_moving)

An fp32 matmul costs 4× a TF32 one. So every fp32 tap I can demote to TF32 saves three-quarters of that tap's cycles. The catch is correctness: the fp32 test tolerance is rtol=1e-5, and TF32's 10-bit mantissa introduces ~1e-3 relative error per multiply. You don't get many taps before you blow the budget.

So I treated it like a budget. I built a separate stress harness that runs the kernel against PyTorch's conv2d across 30 RNG seeds drawn from the tester's exact input distribution, and records the worst-case ratio of observed error to the allowed tolerance:

python · stress_check.py
diff       = np.abs(Y_kernel - Y_torch)        # per-element error
abs_budget = atol + rtol * np.abs(Y_torch)   # per-element tolerance
rel_use    = (diff / abs_budget).max()        # worst over all positions
# rel_use = 1.0 means we exactly graze the ceiling at the worst pixel

My rule: anything over ~0.7 of budget I'd only accept for a sizeable win, and I'd reject anything grazing 1.0. A single TF32 corner tap on the fp32 3×3 case lands at 0.91 of budget — right on the edge, but it passes 30 seeds, so it stays. A second full TF32 tap doubles the error to ~180% of budget and fails outright. I tested four different positions for that second tap; all of them fail. One tap it is.

The prettiest version of this is Case D, which has two input-channel tiles. That means I can apply TF32 to only ci_t=0 and leave the ci_t=1 matmuls in pure fp32. Each output pixel's error then scales with how many TF32 taps hit the first tile, not the total — so I get to spend the budget far more carefully:

Each tap is worth about 10µs. A fifth would cross 100% on at least one seed, so I stopped at four. The X-side cast had its own wrinkle — caching all eight batches in TF32 overflowed SBUF by 832 bytes, so I used a single 2KB shared buffer that gets overwritten per batch, since the Tensor Engine consumes it immediately after each cast anyway:

python · shared TF32 scratch
x_tf32 = nl.ndarray((nl.par_dim(TILE_CI), ROW_BLOCK, out_width),
                  dtype=nl.tfloat32, buffer=nl.sbuf)
for s in nl.static_range(8):
    x_tf32[...] = nisa.tensor_copy(bigs[s][v_c, kh + v_r, v_w + kw], dtype=nl.tfloat32)
    acc_buf[s] += nisa.nc_matmul(Wt_tf32, x_tf32)   # consumed before next overwrite

This single technique — exact-shape-gated, stress-validated TF32 — accounts for the final stretch of the climb, the iterations that took the mean score from 1.8361 to 1.8392.

06The graveyard

Half the value of a project like this is the list of things that didn't work, because each closed door sharpens your model of the machine. A few favorites:

  • Mixed-dtype matmul (TF32 weights, fp32 input) — rejected with NCC_IBIR034. Both operands must share precision, which is why a TF32 tap means casting both sides.
  • PE tiling — splitting the 128-row stationary matrix into two 64-row tiles. Cost is bound by max(…, N_moving), so splitting the stationary dimension buys nothing when the moving dimension is full-size. It cost +150µs.
  • Multi-core SPMD — kernel[nl.nc(2)](...) fails on trn1: "SPMD grid with multi neuron cores is not supported." That's a trn2 feature. One core it is.
  • Tuning instruction order — block-order, drain-order, and tap-order sweeps were all ±2µs noise, because skip_middle_end_transformations schedules matmuls from the dependency graph, not source order. The compiler genuinely doesn't care what order I wrote them in.
  • An 8th TF32 tap on the 5×5 case — passed stress at 97% of budget. Technically a pass. But 97% on 30 seeds is exactly the kind of thing that fails on seed 31, so I capped it at 7.
Meta-lesson On a stack where you've deliberately disabled the optimizer, the compiler stops being an adversary you trick and starts being a contract you fulfill. Once the dependency graph is right, source-level fiddling is pure noise — the real levers are memory layout, bank residency, and precision.

07The last 0.0035

I closed 93% of the gap to the leaderboard top and then stalled, ~30µs short. My honest read on the remainder: it's most likely a precision trick tuned to the tester's single seed that wouldn't survive my 30-seed stress harness — a kernel that passes the grader by grazing the tolerance on exactly the inputs the grader uses, where mine deliberately keeps margin. I could probably claw back the points by being less careful. I decided I'd rather ship a kernel that's correct across the whole distribution than one that's overfit to seed 152.

That feels like the right place to stop: at a deep local optimum, with a clear map of every wall I hit getting here.
